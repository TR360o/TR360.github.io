Brieven uit Maastricht

Embark on a design journey into Limburg's social history, where our team revitalized archives through a vibrant history festival. Focused on pivotal events like the Buckriders, coal mines, and WWII, our concept humanizes history through the lens of letters.

I participated in a graphic design and user experience project for the Social History Center Limburg (SHCL) situated in the province of Limburg, The Netherlands. Despite housing an extensive collection of archives and records chronicling the social history of the region, this historical wealth has not garnered substantial recognition. Within our team of three, my role was to help come up with concepts for the project as well as developing a design aimed at rekindling interest in Limburg's history, ultimately seeking to secure increased support for the SHCL.

We were presented with the challenge of creating a history festival centered around Maastricht and other regions of the Limburg province. After careful consideration, it was decided not to narrow our focus to a specific part of Limburg's history but rather to spotlight significant events throughout its historical timeline. Three pivotal points in time were chosen to be the central focus of the festival: the Buckriders (de Bokkenrijders), the coal mines, and the Second World War. The common thread connecting these events is letters.

Letters, serving as a means of communication from loved ones to family and friends, held immense significance for many people. Individuals poured their hearts into these letters, sharing a myriad of stories. Our concept aims to transport you back in time through the letters that have documented history. By using these letters, we seek to humanize and make these historical events more tangible, providing a unique perspective on the Buckriders, the coal mines, and the Second World War.

Screen shot van AGRA

Each historical point in time was assigned a color, and an icon was created to represent the event. To contrast the weighty topics at hand, it was decided to employ a light and bright color palette. Despite the serious nature of the topics, the festival's design couldn't be too gloomy and should remain accessible for people of all ages. Festival postcards, postage stamps, stickers, and buttons were designed with the idea that visitors could write and send letters to their loved ones, sharing stories.
